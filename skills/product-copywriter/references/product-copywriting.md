# Product Copywriting Reference

Use these principles when a product page, homepage, or positioning pass needs more than a light proofread.

## Research Takeaways

- Web users scan. NN/g found concise, scannable, objective writing performed better than promotional writing in measured usability. Apply this by shortening paragraphs, front-loading meaning, and making headings informative.
- Landing page copy should center the customer's journey, not the company's story. Stripe's Atlas guide recommends rewriting company-first copy around what the visitor sees, needs, and can do.
- Plain language helps expert users too. ONS guidance says even specialist audiences prefer clear language, short sentences, consistent terms, and active voice.
- For specialist audiences, avoid meaningless jargon but keep meaningful domain terms. NN/g distinguishes terminology that helps experts from jargon that hides meaning.

## Practical Editing Heuristics

### Specificity Ladder

When a line feels generic, climb this ladder until it clicks:

1. Category: what kind of product is this?
2. User: who is it for?
3. Situation: when does the user need it?
4. Mechanism: what does the product actually do?
5. Outcome: what changes because of that?
6. Proof: what evidence makes the claim believable?

Example:

- Weak: "A powerful platform for modern teams."
- Better: "Collect every revision, approval, and sign-off in one review queue."

### Common Rewrites

- "Unlock insights" -> "See which accounts changed behavior this week."
- "Streamline workflows" -> "Route each request to the right reviewer and show what is waiting."
- "AI-powered" -> name the useful behavior or remove it.
- "Seamless" -> name the integration, handoff, or missing step.
- "Transform how teams..." -> name the specific before and after.

### Good Product Copy Usually Has

- A clear noun: the thing being sold or explained.
- A strong verb: what the product does.
- A concrete object: data, files, reviews, reports, requests, schedules, or blockers.
- A reader-visible result: fewer missed steps, shorter review cycles, clearer ownership, or faster decisions.

### Copy Tension

Strong copy is not always shorter; it is both more dense and easier to understand. Keep details that make the product legible; cut filler that only adds polish.
